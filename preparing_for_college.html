/ here's the sitch

Your high school career doesn't determine the outcome of your future, but take advantage.
In recent years, academic achievement has become more important than ever before. Pressure for good grades, whether it’s from your parents or teachers or even from yourself, can leave students incredibly stressed. Not to mention, the stigma of a well-rounded resume can cause students to be completely consumed with costly standardized test preparation programs, challenging college-level classes, and demanding extracurriculars. Just like you, we have experience going through this process and a lot of the same things. No matter how you take on your junior and senior years, just make sure you are not overdoing yourself. Give yourself room to breath, room to prosper, and room to enjoy this time before this next chapter in life.

/ prepare and take standardized tests

Don’t wait until the fall of your senior year to take the SAT and ACT and any other standardized tests.
Taking them as early as possible is key to finding out what you need to improve on as well as giving you a better chance to get scholarships depending on how good your scores are. The very last time I would suggest taking either is in October of your senior year; because all applications are due by the first of November, you can then send your scores to your preferred colleges and superscore.

For preparing -- KhanAcademy is free and a great resource. I personally used it a lot for math, but it can be used for any other subject too. Based on the date of your test, it would set daily reminders to practice a given number of questions, including practice tests which serve as mock exams. The Princeton Review is also a great help. The books and flashcards are reasonable prices, and are a great way to study if you prefer to do your work on paper. I used these to work on my vocabulary and overall reading strategies.

/ research colleges and attend college fairs

Start thinking about your near future, whether it involves college, a gap year or jumping right into your career.
Start thinking about college if that is what you’re considering for the future. Consider taking a college road tour over spring break to get a feel for schools in a certain area. Make sure the whole range of schools is considered -- public, private, large, and small -- and take a look at what majors they offer, as well as any other factors that you are looking for, and pay close attention to those. College fairs in your city or nearby can be great places for asking questions, being that the people at each table tend to be alumni or current students at the school they are representing.

For those looking to be recruited for a sport -- make a highlights video, reach out to coaches and give them your school or club schedule, go to ID camps, schedule appointments. Do not feel bummed if you don’t get a response back -- coaches are incredibly busy, especially during the recruiting season, but could also not be interested. Make a references section in your resume for coaches you’ve previously had before and who could vouch for your athletic abilities as well as your presence on the team or in the sport. And keep in mind that if you are looking at D3 schools, you will not be able to get an athletic scholarship, but can still apply for a number of academic scholarships.

I am a victim of procrastinating all my work. I’ll come home from practice, procrastinate my shower, procrastinate my assignments, and then procrastinate my sleep. There’s no methodology to preventing this other than just doing it. Keep yourself in check and your grades will thank you. With that being said, though, make sure to form good study habits. Depending on when your next test is, do not cram. Spread your studying over a long period of time, or maybe just do a little reviewing of your notes a day.
